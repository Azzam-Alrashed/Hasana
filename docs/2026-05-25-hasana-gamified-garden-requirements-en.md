# Hasana: Gamified Garden Requirements

Date: 2026-05-25
Language: English
Status: Idea-phase requirements

## 1. Product Vision

Hasana is a gamified habit-building app for young Muslims. It helps users build consistency with obligatory acts and Sunnah practices through a beautiful garden-building experience.

The central idea is simple: the user's worship and spiritual habits grow into a lifelong garden. Each act of worship is represented as a meaningful tree or plant. As the user continues practicing, the garden becomes richer, calmer, and more beautiful.

Hasana should feel encouraging, spiritually grounded, and emotionally gentle. It should motivate without guilt, pressure, or unhealthy comparison.

## 2. Target Audience

The first audience is young Muslims who want to become more consistent in their worship but may need motivation, structure, and a more emotionally engaging experience.

The product should be approachable for users who are still building their habits. It should not assume that every user is already highly consistent, highly knowledgeable, or comfortable with strict tracking.

The tone should feel:

- Warm and hopeful.
- Youth-friendly without being childish.
- Respectful of worship and sincerity.
- Motivating without turning religion into a score-chasing activity.

## 3. Core Metaphor: The Lifelong Garden

The main interface and long-term progression system is a garden.

This garden is not a short daily board that resets completely. It is a lifelong garden that grows with the user over months and years. The garden should represent accumulated spiritual care, consistency, discovery, and beauty.

The garden should communicate progress visually before relying on numbers. The user should be able to look at the garden and feel that their actions have created something meaningful.

Core principles:

- The garden grows through worship and Sunnah practices.
- Growth should feel peaceful and rewarding.
- Missing a day should not destroy the user's progress.
- The app should encourage returning, repairing, and continuing.

## 4. Worship Representation

Each worship act should have a distinct visual representation in the garden.

### Obligatory Acts

Obligatory acts are foundational trees. They give the garden structure, life, and stability.

Examples may include:

- The five daily prayers.
- Fasting Ramadan.
- Zakat where applicable to the user.

These should be treated with care. The experience should not make obligatory worship feel like a game in a shallow way. Instead, the garden metaphor should honor their importance by making them foundational.

### Sunnah Practices

Sunnah practices appear as beautiful plants, flowers, smaller trees, or garden details that add variety and richness.

Examples may include:

- Sunnah prayers.
- Witr.
- Morning and evening adhkar.
- Quran recitation.
- Voluntary fasting.

These practices should make the garden feel more alive and personal.

### Forgotten Sunnahs

Forgotten Sunnahs are rare hidden plants. They are a key differentiator for Hasana.

They should feel like beautiful discoveries rather than extra chores. The user may uncover them through learning, seasonal prompts, or guided challenges.

Design intent:

- Encourage curiosity.
- Teach gently.
- Reward discovery with rare beauty.
- Avoid overwhelming beginners with too many advanced practices at once.

## 5. Classification Model

Each worship item should be classified in two ways.

### Worship Type

The worship type describes what area of worship the item belongs to.

Initial types:

- Prayer.
- Fasting.
- Dhikr.
- Quran.
- Zakat or charity where applicable.

This classification can influence the plant family, color palette, garden area, iconography, and seasonal behavior.

### Religious Status

The religious status describes the ruling or role of the act.

Initial statuses:

- Obligatory.
- Confirmed Sunnah.
- Forgotten Sunnah.
- Seasonal voluntary act.

This classification can influence rarity, prominence, growth behavior, and how the app introduces the act to the user.

## 6. Seasons

Hasana should support both Islamic seasons and personal seasons.

### Islamic Seasons

Islamic seasons can unlock special plants, visual states, reminders, and guided opportunities.

Examples:

- Ramadan.
- Fridays.
- The first ten days of Dhul Hijjah.
- Prayer times.
- Voluntary fasting days.

Seasonal experiences should feel special but not stressful. They should invite the user into opportunity rather than make them feel behind.

### Personal Seasons

Personal seasons are future user-defined challenges or focus periods.

Examples:

- A Quran consistency month.
- A Witr challenge.
- A prayer-on-time focus period.
- A personal reset after a difficult period.

This concept should remain flexible until the MVP is defined.

## 7. Gamification Principles

Hasana should use gamification to create motivation, beauty, and emotional attachment. It should not reduce worship to empty points.

Recommended mechanics:

- Growth stages: seed, sprout, young plant or tree, mature tree, flowering state.
- Accumulated beauty: light, flowers, water, ambience, gentle animation, and environmental richness.
- Discovery: hidden Sunnahs and rare plants.
- Collection: meaningful plants tied to worship categories and seasons.
- Gentle continuity: encourage consistency while allowing recovery.

Avoid:

- Harsh failure states.
- Public ranking based on worship.
- Shame-driven streak loss.
- Excessive numerical scoring.
- Competition that may harm sincerity.

The primary reward should be seeing the garden flourish.

## 8. Social Features

The final vision includes limited friends, but social features should remain gentle and privacy-first.

Possible friend interactions:

- Send encouragement.
- Send a dua.
- Send a symbolic seed or garden gift.
- Celebrate progress without exposing sensitive worship details.

Privacy principles:

- Worship details should be private by default.
- Friends should not automatically see exact acts completed.
- Social features should avoid comparison and public pressure.
- The user should control what is visible.

The social layer should support encouragement, not surveillance.

## 9. Open Questions

These questions should remain open until deeper product, religious, and user experience research is done.

### Completion Logging

How should the user record that they completed a worship act?

Possible directions:

- Self-reporting based on trust.
- Logging with a short reflection.
- Time-aware logging for time-bound acts.
- A mixed model depending on the worship type.

This decision needs more research because it affects sincerity, emotional comfort, accuracy, and friction.

### Sincerity and Motivation

How can the app motivate without making worship feel transactional?

Questions to explore:

- When should the app reward the user?
- How much should the app show progress numerically?
- How can the app encourage intention before action?
- How should it respond when the user misses a habit?

### Worship Catalog

How detailed should the worship catalog be?

Questions to explore:

- Which acts are included at launch?
- Which acts require user-specific eligibility?
- How are differences in scholarly opinions handled?
- Which forgotten Sunnahs should be introduced first?

### Religious Review

Some content should not be finalized without religious review.

Areas likely needing review:

- Worship definitions.
- Classification of Sunnahs.
- Seasonal recommendations.
- Wording of religious guidance.
- How gamification is framed around obligatory acts.

## 10. Current Assumptions

- Hasana is for young Muslims first.
- The current product scope is obligatory acts and Sunnah practices.
- Hasana is not a general productivity, wellness, or life-management app.
- The final experience is a personal lifelong garden.
- Friends exist only as a limited encouragement layer.
- Forgotten Sunnahs are a major product differentiator.
- The app should be gentle, beautiful, and spiritually respectful.
- This document is not an MVP implementation spec.
